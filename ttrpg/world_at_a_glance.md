# ESTAEA
The continent of Estaea is not a comfortable place. Its geography has a way of making ambition expensive: mountain ranges that wall off entire regions, rivers that carry trade for a hundred miles and then stop at a forest no one enters, coastlines that drop into the sea without offering a harbor for days of sailing. The nations that have risen here have done so in spite of the land as much as because of it, and they carry the marks of that effort.

The western coast belongs to Coudassis, the oldest human nation on the continent, its capital Alqasar Gurdiana sitting at the end of a river mouth where a dormant volcano has been slowly rearranging the harbor floor for centuries. The republic spreads inland from that coast, bounded north by the Argash Arms and east by the Marenthal River, which marks the border with the Bellicosian Empire. Along that river’s western bank runs the Gallaghan Protectorate, a militarized strip of territory that Bellicosian armies held briefly during the Coudassian civil war and Coudassian forces bled to retake. It is administered separately from the princedoms, closer to a permanent military installation than a settled province. The southernmost reach of Coudassian territory ends at the White Cliff Cape, where the land simply stops, shearing off into the Sea of Beresheim far below.

The Bellicosian Empire fills the broad Charonith Plains at the center of the continent, its territory running from the Argash Arms in the north to the Arbican Delta in the south, where the rice crop that feeds the empire grows in the flat, river-threaded ground near Pandare Bay. Lake Casimire sits in the shadow of the mountains near the western edge of imperial territory; the Ilasan Citadel, the imperial capital, occupies the plains somewhere between that lake and the delta, more or less at the center of everything the empire claims. The Castellan Mountains rise in the southwest, a further boundary between Bellicosian and Coudassian territory where the two nations press against each other most uncomfortably.

East of the empire, past the Tandu Plains and the Reshi Delta where the frontier grows porous and difficult to police, lies the Kingdom of Meresaw. Griffin’s Keep sits on the northern coast where the Straights of Rhys open into the Niaterre Sea, a harbor city large enough to be visible from a considerable distance. South of it, Athore’s Foot feeds the Elisumnaessavia River, which runs southward through the kingdom until it reaches Llewelyn Wood and disappears. The Wood sits at roughly the midpoint of the continent, ancient enough that no human alive remembers a time before it, and warded in ways that have resisted every attempt at understanding. South of the Wood the river re-emerges and continues toward the Gulf of Eristae, passing through country that Meresaw claims and the Reshi tribes administer. The Lockabier Peaks form the kingdom’s eastern wall, their ridgelines too jagged for any practical pass, marking the division between Meresagian territory and what lies beyond.
What lies beyond is the Far Eastern Grasslands, the territory of Alworum Nauroytaira. The grass runs from the Lockabier Peaks to the Echartean Sea, wide and rolling and tall, covering ground that was forest before the dwarven armies moved through it. The Gulf of Kannissor opens along the northeastern coast, and Baelessaira, the elven capital, sits at the eastern edge of what was once the heart of elvenkind’s empire. The Isle of Goldwynne lies offshore in the Echartean Sea, visible from the coast on clear days.

The continent’s southern edge is less settled and less understood. The Onseth Jungles run along the southeastern coast, and the Shadow Wood and Black Dagger Hills occupy the uncertain ground between the Meresagian frontier and the jungle. The Corsvent Swamp and Tan Marl mark the southern edge of the Reshi Delta, and the Salingar Reefs make the coastal waters treacherous for vessels that do not know them. The Harpway Channel separates that coast from Echartea, the island empire whose ships move through every sea lane on the continent and whose interest in the mainland’s affairs has never been purely commercial.

In the northwest, beyond the Demarkland River that marks the edge of Coudassian territory, lie the Cainus Wilderlands: frontier country of uncertain governance, bounded by Caigwich Bay and the cold Niaterre Sea to the north. The Fens of the Dead occupy the northeastern corner of that territory. The name has discouraged thorough investigation.
The rivers thread through all of it. The Marenthal divides Coudassis from Bellicose and has been the site of more skirmishes than either nation officially acknowledges. The Elisumnaessavia links the great northern lake to the southern sea, or would, if the Wood did not interrupt it. The rivers of the Arbican Delta feed the empire’s southern breadbasket. They are the continent’s arteries, and like most arteries, they are worth fighting over.

## THE REPUBLIC OF COUDASSIS
Coudassis occupies the southwestern reaches of Estaea, from the Argash Arms mountain range in the north to the sheer coastal cliffs of the southern sea, and east to the Marenthal River, which forms its contested border with the Bellicosian Empire. It is the oldest of the human nations on the continent, and it carries that age the way old families carry old money: conspicuously, and with considerable anxiety about losing it.

The republic began as an empire. Its soldiers once marched as far as ambition and supply lines allowed, and for a time that was quite far. When the Bellicosian Empire rose to contest them, expansion stopped; when the resulting stalemate bred resentment at home, civil war followed. The empire fractured into forty-two independent princedoms, and it was the sustained pressure of Bellicosian armies that eventually forced those princedoms to cooperate. The Republic of Coudassis emerged from that necessity, its constitution written to ensure that no single princedom could dominate the others. Whether it has succeeded in that aim depends entirely on whom you ask.

### Government
Coudassis is a crowned republic, though the crown sits lighter than the title suggests. The king is elected from among the princes by the Lord’s Senate and holds the position for life; he is chosen, in practice, for his willingness to remain largely decorative. His primary constitutional function is to cast deciding votes when the senate reaches an impasse, which is often. Real power moves through two legislative bodies: the Lord’s Senate, composed of all forty-two princes, and the Common Senate, whose elected representatives serve single ten-year terms on behalf of the merchant and noble classes. Neither branch trusts the other, and both are correct not to.

The guilds wield influence that rivals many of the lesser princes. The Merchants’ Guild, the Craftsmen’s Guild, the Scholars’ Guild, the Artists’ Guild, the Mercenaries’ Guild, and others each hold seats in the Common Senate through sponsorship arrangements, and a prince who wishes something done will often find it more efficient to negotiate with a guild master than to navigate the senate floor. The League of Antiquities, dedicated to the recovery and preservation of ancient artifacts, occupies a particular position of respect, its members welcomed by powerful interests across the republic and beyond its borders.

The political culture of Coudassis rewards observation and punishes transparency. Every gesture of a noble carries presumed subtext; what is left unsaid is analyzed as carefully as what is spoken. Visitors who mistake Coudassian pleasantry for warmth tend to regret it, and those who mistake Coudassian formality for weakness tend to regret it more.

### The Gallaghan Protectorate
East of the Marenthal River lies the Gallaghan Protectorate, a collection of city-states administered separately from the republic proper. The protectorate was established by General Gallaghan following the Second Bellicosian War, when Coudassian armies had pushed back across the river and reclaimed lost territory. Rather than return that territory to the princes who had funded the campaign, Gallaghan declared martial law, named his commanders as Martial Governors, and presented the king and senate with a fait accompli. The arrangement was eventually regularized: the city-states now elect lords from the central senate, a war council called the Conclave oversees military affairs in the region, and an uneasy armistice holds the Bellicosian border.

The armistice has not ended conflict. Raids across the Marenthal for livestock, slaves, and occasionally land are frequent enough to be considered seasonal. The forts and city-states of the protectorate exist in a state of permanent, low-grade war that the republic officially regards as peacetime.

### People and Culture
Coudassians are defined, above most things, by their investment in appearance. Fashion is not merely vanity here; it is communication. The length of a man’s title, the quality of his doublet, the particular cut of a noblewoman’s collar: all of it is read, and read carefully, by everyone in the room. Men of standing favor doublets with fluted sleeves, damasked velvet, and thigh-high boots regardless of the weather. Women wear high-necked empress dresses with equal indifference to the subtropical heat.
Coudassians range in complexion from the deep nut-brown of the southern coast to the honey and coffee tones of the north. Hair runs dark, eyes most commonly brown or black, though lighter shades appear with some regularity.

Cities throughout the republic follow a grid plan, built primarily from the limestone, soapstone, and granite that the region supplies in abundance. Buildings are flat-roofed, their upper terraces given over to gardens or outdoor gathering. The wealthier districts occupy city centers; the poor are pushed to the margins. Public baths and aqueducts are a point of civic pride, and most cities of any size maintain them well.

The class structure runs from Royal and Noble through Merchant to Peasant. Foreigners and free elves occupy the lower end of the peasant class and find movement upward exceedingly difficult. Slavery is legal and common; indentured servitude more so. Criminal sentences frequently result in bond labor, and the slave markets of the larger cities are busy institutions.

Elves living within the republic are subject to legal restrictions that vary by principality but are universally limiting. Registration is required in most jurisdictions. Elves cannot hold noble rank. Their considerable skills in craft and art are prized by the noble class, which creates a particular precariousness: an elven artisan with a powerful patron lives well; an elven artisan whose patron loses interest may find themselves with few options.

### A Brief History
One hundred and twenty years ago, the Coudassian Empire first encountered the Bellicosian Empire along what would become a contested border. Thirty years of friction ended in civil war at home, and within six years the old empire had fractured into forty-two princedoms. The republic was formalized two years after that, and within another two years the Second Bellicosian War had begun. The Gallaghan Protectorate was established four years into that conflict. The republic has been more or less at war along its eastern border, in one form or another, since.

### The Guilds
Guild membership shapes daily life in Coudassis more than almost any other institution below the level of the senate. The major guilds maintain their own legal representation, their own standards of conduct, and in some cases their own security forces. Sponsorship by a Lord’s Senate prince gives the larger guilds political standing that smaller princedoms would envy.

The Merchants’ Guild controls the flow of trade through the republic with a thoroughness that amounts to informal taxation. Its membership is exclusive and its network extensive; a merchant operating outside it will find certain doors closed that no amount of talent will open. The Craftsmen’s Guild sets quality standards and apprenticeship terms for every skilled trade, from blacksmithing to masonry to weaving. The Scholars’ Guild oversees the republic’s institutions of learning and research, and its members are among the most widely traveled people in Coudassis, since the pursuit of knowledge is difficult to confine to one princedom. The Artists’ Guild operates on patronage: noble and wealthy sponsors commission work and confer status; talented artists without sponsors find the guild’s doors only slightly more open than those of the Merchants.
The Mercenaries’ Guild provides what might be called organized military labor: trained soldiers available for hire, operating under a code of conduct that makes them predictable enough to trust with valuable work. They are not universally admired, but in a republic where the princes maintain their own armies and the protectorate is perpetually understaffed, reliable contracted soldiers fill a real need.

The League of Antiquities occupies a distinct position among the republic’s organizations. Its members pursue ancient artifacts and historical knowledge across Estaea, operating with a degree of official sanction that smooths their access to ruins and restricted sites. They are well-regarded, well-funded, and genuinely useful to powerful people, which is perhaps the most durable form of safety available in Coudassis.

### Navigating the Republic
Titles matter and must be used correctly. Every person of standing maintains a full title, a formal title, a short title for closer acquaintances, and familial titles reserved for family. Using the wrong form of address is not a trivial error; it communicates either ignorance or intentional disrespect, and Coudassians will assume the latter before they assume the former. Foreigners are given a degree of latitude on this, but less than they typically expect.

Bribery is not a corruption of the system. It is the system. Services at every level of Coudassian society, from a harbor official processing cargo manifests to a senator’s secretary scheduling an audience, operate on the understanding that appropriate consideration will accompany any request. Attempting to conduct business without this understanding will result in delays that no amount of impatience resolves.

The criminal enterprise that operates alongside legitimate Coudassian institutions is organized enough to be, in certain respects, predictable. Merchants and nobles of standing are generally left alone; this immunity is understood to be a courtesy extended by the underworld rather than a protection afforded by the law, and it functions as long as both parties behave accordingly. Visitors who find themselves needing to navigate this layer of Coudassian life are advised to find someone who already knows how before attempting it themselves.

Magic users are required to register with the state and carry documentation of their training. Enforcement varies by princedom, stricter in the capital region and considerably more relaxed in frontier areas of the protectorate, but the requirement exists everywhere in the republic. Unregistered arcanists who attract attention will find Coudassis unwelcoming.

When a new title or honorific is bestowed, it must be witnessed by at least three senators of the appropriate senate, who formally attest to the title’s legitimacy. The process is deliberate, expensive, and taken entirely seriously. A Coudassian noble’s full name, recited at a formal greeting, may take a perceptible amount of time to deliver. This is considered a mark of distinction rather than an inconvenience, at least by the person being greeted.

### Cities of the Republic
**Alqasar Gurdiana —** The capital sits at the end of the Cudro River where it empties into Arapejo Bay, a natural harbor shaped by the mouth of a dormant volcano. The bay floor has been rising and falling for centuries as magma shifts beneath it; portions of the old city now lie submerged, their upper stories converted to low-cost lodging or repurposed as fish wells. The city extends roughly two miles along the coastline and a mile inland, climbing toward the Imperial Palace on its central rise. The palace has housed every head of state since the empire’s founding. Around the harbor, floating markets of joined boats and flat-bottomed barges serve as storefronts, taverns, and less reputable establishments. The canal district beyond it is built on pilings, its lower floors designed with the expectation of seasonal flooding. The great market of the trade district will sell nearly anything to nearly anyone. Population: approximately 200,000.

**Labrija — **The largest city of the Gallaghan Protectorate sits on Pandare Bay within the Sea of Bersheim, built at the foot of a mountain fortress that predates the republic by an age. The fortress itself was constructed on the remains of a first men stronghold; below it, cut into the bedrock, are catacombs and passages whose original purpose has been forgotten. The city serves as the primary military supply hub for the Bellicosian border, and its army ward is unlike anything else in Coudassis: five generals may be in residence simultaneously, each commanding their own forces, the soldiers competing visibly for favor and advancement. The trade ward is among the wealthiest in the republic, stocked with goods that the more regulated markets of the interior do not carry. The eldest son of the current king serves as City Lord. Population: approximately 50,000.
 
## THE BELLICOSIAN EMPIRE

The Bellicosian Empire spreads across the Charonith Plains at the center of Estaea’s contested middle ground, reaching from the Argash Arms in the north to the Arbican Delta at the southern coast. It is flanked by Coudassis to the west and Meresaw to the east, a position that shapes everything about how the Empire conducts itself: bounded by rivals on two sides, sustained by discipline, and utterly certain of its own eventual supremacy.

Where Coudassis governs through negotiation and Meresaw through tradition, Bellicose governs through the state. Every citizen has a place assigned by birth, talent, and service. Every place carries obligations. The machinery runs because its people believe it should, and that belief is cultivated from childhood with a thoroughness that outsiders tend to find unsettling and Bellicosians tend to find unremarkable.

### Government
Bellicose is a stratocracy: those who have served the state most ably hold the most power, and military rank is the surest path to both. The caste structure that organizes Bellicosian life descends from the Royal Family at its apex through the Aristocracy, the Martials, the Ashan priests, the Military, Peasants, Artisans, and Merchants, to Servants and Slaves at its base. The placement of Peasants above Artisans and Merchants is not accidental; those who feed the empire rank above those who merely profit from it.

The current Emperor, D’Atrenos, rose to power forty years ago in circumstances the empire now calls the Year of the Godsent. He executed the old royal family, rebuilt the imperial seat in his own image, and has governed since with an authority that does not appear to diminish with age. He is the Grand Martial and the Emperor in one person; the distinction that once existed between military command and civil rule no longer does. His birthday is observed as a religious occasion. His statues line the halls of every significant public building in the empire. Outsiders tend to read this as vanity. Bellicosians tend to read it as theology.

The functions of government are divided among ministries: Defense, Infrastructure, Trade, the Interior, Education, Science, Justice, and Bounty, among others. Each ministry is headed by an Aristocrat and reaches into daily life with a thoroughness that keeps the empire running and its citizens watched.

### The Faith
Alkira is the only deity officially recognized within the Bellicosian Empire. The particular form of worship practiced here is called Ja’Alkira, and it differs from the broader Estaean veneration of the pantheon in one important respect: it is singular. Where a common worshipper elsewhere might light a candle to Ambis for a safe birth or invoke Rudlu before a gamble, the Bellicosian turns to Alkira for all things.

Ja’Alkira is not, despite appearances, a religion of war. Its central teaching is that all life is precious; war is permitted because order sometimes requires it, but death is never the goal. Followers of Ja’Alkira are strict vegetarians, and the killing of animals for food is considered an abhorrence. The faith applies these restraints primarily to its own followers; outsiders may find the consideration less reliably extended to them.

The Ashan are the priests of Ja’Alkira, divided into two orders: the N’Ashan, who heal and enforce the law of Alkira on the battlefield, and the Y’Ashan, who bring destruction in his name. Both serve as commissars within the military, operating outside the standard chain of command. Arcane magic is outlawed across the empire; thirty years ago, in what the empire calls the Year of Magi, Emperor D’Atrenos proscribed it entirely. Arcanists discovered within the empire are most often executed. The Ashan enforce this with thoroughness, which leaves them as the only available conduit for anything resembling magical power in Bellicosian society.

### What It Is to Be Bellicosian
Every child born in the empire begins a pilgrimage on their sixteenth birthday: a journey on foot to the Ilasan Citadel, the imperial capital, where the first stage of state service begins. The pilgrimage is deliberate. It builds self-reliance, exposes young citizens to the country they will be asked to defend, and ensures that those who arrive have already demonstrated they can manage hardship. Children who fail to arrive, for whatever reason, are designated Enkleen: forsaken, marked, and assigned to the lowest rungs of state service with little prospect of advancement.

State service runs from sixteen to twenty-four. The first stage, Inception, strips away everything particular about the individual: hair is shaved, identical garments are issued, and the doctrines of the state are instilled with patient thoroughness. Foundation follows, sorting those suited to military life from those better suited to other roles. Formation provides the skilled trade training that the state has determined each person requires. At its conclusion, young adults enter Bellicosian society equipped for a specific function, honored for fulfilling it well, and aware that the state’s eye remains upon them.

Women in Bellicose are partners and equals in household and commerce. The Ahdam, the matriarch of a family or business, holds recognized authority within her domain. Women of marriageable age obscure their faces: younger women paint the upper half, while those twenty-four and older wear headdresses of varying elaboration. The most significant of these, the Adham, is a family heirloom passed from matriarch to matriarch, its cleverly disguised eyeholes invisible to the uninitiated.

Bellicosians receive foreigners with what they call Respectable Distance: courteous, correct, and entirely without warmth. The cool detachment that characterizes these encounters is not rudeness by Bellicosian standards; it is the appropriate register for dealing with someone outside the community of obligation.

### A Brief History
Fifty years ago, a failed rice harvest brought famine to the empire. Three years later, families throughout Bellicose were required to offer a child born during the famine years as a sacrifice to the state. Forty years ago, the Year of the Godsent: D’Atrenos takes power, the old royal family is executed, and the empire remakes itself around its new emperor. Thirty years ago, arcane magic is outlawed. The empire since has been stable, disciplined, and expanding by increments along every border it shares with its neighbors.

### Cities of the Empire
**Ilasan Citadel —** The imperial capital sits at the heart of the Charonith Plains, its walls rebuilt by D’Atrenos to reflect his vision of the state: orderly, impressive, and oriented entirely around the Emperor’s supremacy. The palace ward at its center houses statues of D’Atrenos in every corridor; alongside them, in the same shrines, stands Alkira. The Inception Ward receives the empire’s sixteen-year-olds each year, its austere white walls and sand gardens having processed more citizens than any other institution in Bellicose. Trade within the city is carefully regulated; the sale of meat is forbidden within the citadel walls, and black-clad enforcers patrol the trade ward to ensure compliance. Population: approximately 500,000.

**Armanjska —** Founded with military intent a hundred and fifty years ago at the mouth of the Ehlasenk River, Armanjska commands the Arbican Delta, the empire’s primary rice-producing region. The city’s gatehouse rises in tiers above a gateway wide enough for eight lanes of traffic; its walls reach forty feet. The trade ward outside the main city gates accommodates merchants and non-citizens, who are not permitted entry to the city proper. Inside, the wards are orderly and austere, the streets wide enough to parade entire units through for inspection. The Temple of Amber, its columns and walls decorated in mosaic tiles of every shade of amber, is considered one of the architectural treasures of the continent. Population: approximately 500,000.
 
## THE KINGDOM OF MERESAW

The Kingdom of Meresaw occupies the heartland of Estaea, a broad and prosperous realm wedged between the Bellicosian Empire to the west and the open grasslands of the Far East. It is a kingdom that feeds the continent, breeds the finest horses on it, and has managed, through sixty-seven successive reigns of a single family, to remain standing between two powers that would each prefer it didn’t.

Meresaw is not a subtle nation. Its virtues are legible: honest government, military service as genuine honor rather than obligation, and a relationship with the Elves old enough to have become something other than diplomacy. What complexity it possesses tends to live in the gap between what the kingdom aspires to and what the pressures on its borders permit.

### The Land
Nothing shapes Meresaw more completely than Llewelyn Wood. The ancient forest dominates a great swathe of the kingdom’s interior, older than any human civilization on the continent and warded by magics that have never weakened in all the centuries since. No one enters uninvited; no one who has done so has returned. The kingdom learned this early and has not required the lesson twice.

What makes the Wood more than a forbidden place is its geography. It crosses the Elisumnaessavia River, the great waterway that runs south from Athore’s Foot toward the sea, breaking the river as a trade artery at precisely the point where it would matter most. Goods must be moved overland around the forest’s edges, at considerable cost in time and labor. In practical terms, Llewelyn Wood divides Meresaw into two halves: a settled and prosperous north around Athore’s Foot and the capital, and a southern reach that the crown governs at distance and with difficulty.

The northern half is the kingdom’s strength. Athore’s Foot, the vast freshwater lake from which the Elisumnaessavia is born, anchors the settled heartland and gives Meresaw control of trade, agriculture, and fresh water across the region. No king of Meresaw has forgotten what that leverage is worth.
South of the Wood, the kingdom reasserts itself around Devil’s Fork, a fortified city on a large island in the Elisumnaessavia where the river emerges from the forest’s shadow. South of it, the land belongs in practice to the Reshi tribes, whose roots predate any Meresagian presence. An uneasy alliance holds: the Reshi keep their autonomy; Meresaw keeps its river access to the southern coast. To the east, the Lockabier Peaks seal the kingdom off from the Far Eastern grasslands, home to the Urjack tribesmen whose indifference to the affairs of the lowlands is absolute.

### Origins and the Crown
The Meresagians came from across the sea, refugees who left a homeland deliberately and without intention of return. The family that shaped what followed was the Dermonts, led by Meresaw Dermont himself, who organized the first settlements and held competing clans together through the worst years. His son Merrin discovered Athore’s Foot and the wild horses that ranged around it; the crossbreeding program he began, combining those animals with the heavy warhorses brought from the homeland, produced the line for which Meresaw is now known across the continent.

In the fifth year of settlement, a lone Elvish scout encountered the newcomers and was struck, according to the old accounts, not by their numbers but by the quality of their horses. That meeting led eventually to a formal gathering; when the emissary asked to speak with whoever led these new men and Meresaw Dermont stepped forward, the Elf declared him a king. The country has borne his name since. Sixty-seven kings of the Dermont line have reigned since.

The current king is Envince Dermont, the eighth child and only son of his father Alfred. His seven elder sisters, each prepared for governance by a father who spent his reign building the conditions for women to lead in substance where they could not lead in title, govern the kingdom’s seven provinces as Duchesses. The court is warm, capable, and shaped by the particular knowledge each of Envince’s sisters carries: that the throne would have been hers under any other circumstance.

### Military
Service to the crown in Meresaw is an honor, not a compulsion, and the distinction matters to how soldiers carry themselves. All swear their oaths to the king personally. At sixteen, young men and women throughout the kingdom face an annual contest of skill that functions as both coming-of-age ritual and military assessment. Noble birth purchases no exemption from the standard. The phrase spoken at every such contest has become something close to a national motto: To each man the strength to uplift a kingdom, to each kingdom a legion of men strong enough to carry it.

The Order of Leichtern is the finest military institution the kingdom has produced: elite knights trained across the full spectrum of mounted combat with particular mastery of long polearms. Women have served in the Order for generations, since King Aldric opened its ranks in a moment of practical necessity that settled into cultural expectation within a generation. A female knight of the Order is no curiosity; she is simply a knight. Every knight learns the Elven language; for the Elves, the willingness to speak their tongue is an act of respect that no treaty alone could replicate.

### People and Culture
Meresagians are fair-skinned, with hair ranging from black and brown to red and blonde, and eyes that tend toward blue, green, grey, and the paler browns. The kingdom’s temperate heartland is well-suited to agriculture, and Meresaw is the primary supplier of grain to the nations of Estaea. Every citizen rides. The horse is the kingdom’s defining cultural symbol as much as a practical tool, and the crossbred Meresagian horse is exported at prices that reflect its reputation.

Meresagian nobility favors a long-sleeved coat with a mandarin collar, cut to mid-thigh, with the house device embroidered on the left breast and rank indicated through degrees of gilding and embroidery that every Meresagian courtier reads fluently. No nobleman appears in public without his sword; it is worn to banquets and funerals alike, and the quality of the blade speaks to the house’s history as plainly as any coat of arms.

Meresaw holds the largest concentration of Elves living among humans of any nation outside the Far Eastern grasslands. The alliance is old enough to have produced genuine mutual understanding. A dedicated Elven detachment within the capital’s city guard attends to matters of Elvish law, an arrangement that has functioned without significant friction since the kingdom’s earliest years.

### Foreign Relations
Relations with the Bellicosian Empire are cool without being actively hostile. The Empire regards Meresagian territory as a long-term objective; the kingdom’s heavy cavalry has spent generations reminding them why that objective is expensive. The southern frontier, where the Tandu Plains break into ravines and plateaus, is subject to Bellicosian incursions regular enough to be treated as seasonal. The theological dimension of this pressure complicates any diplomatic resolution; Bellicose regards the southern delta as a matter of divine obligation, and a faith is considerably harder to satisfy with a border treaty than an emperor.

Meresaw and Coudassis maintain a neutrality agreement that has kept the two nations from adding to the conflicts already burning along their other borders. The arrangement is practical rather than warm: Coudassis is already stretched along the Marenthal, and Meresaw has the Bellicosian frontier to manage without opening another. One of the Coudassis princedoms has been angling for preferential trade route access through Meresagian territory, offering armed ships in Meresagian harbors as a deterrent against pirates. Whether Envince considers that offer worth the entanglement remains an open question at court.

### Cities of the Kingdom
**Griffin’s Keep —** The capital is the largest fortified city in the world, spanning a natural harbor more than three miles across and rising on the landward side to a central Keep occupying over a square mile of elevated ground. The city was built on the ruins of a dwarven stronghold, and the infrastructure beneath it is dwarven work: sewers and aqueducts modified and extended over centuries but never fundamentally improved upon. The harbor stays clean. The city does not smell the way most cities of its size smell, and its residents are quietly proud of this. The trade district operates through the night, and Griffin’s Keep has a reputation as the one city in Estaea where anything can be found by someone willing to look. Escaped slaves from other nations are not extradited, and Coudassians and Bellicosians have been known to socialize in the same tavern while their countries maintain a state of nominal war.

**Devil’s Fork —** Built on a large island in the Elisumnaessavia where the river emerges from the shadow of Llewelyn Wood, Devil’s Fork is simultaneously a distribution hub, a military installation, and the southern kingdom’s primary point of contact with the outside world. Eight bridges span the river to both banks, arching high enough for the largest river vessels to pass beneath. Troops rotate through constantly, moving between the island and the forts that line the southern frontier. The ancient catacombs beneath the island, carved by the First Men and claimed by the crown, have made Devil’s Fork a steady source of relics and artifacts for those willing to go looking. Population: approximately 80,000.
 
## The Far-Eastern Grasslands (ALWORUM NAUROYTAIRA)
The vast rolling expanse east of the Lockabier Peaks is called Alworum Nauroytaira by its inhabitants, the New Elven Kingdom, though the name carries more grief than grandeur. What stretches across that land today, a wide sea of grass growing taller than a man, broken only by the occasional copse of trees or bend of river, was once unbroken forest: ancient woodland that ran from the Elisumnaessavia River to the eastern coast, blessed under Hirnaloyta, older than any human presence on the continent. The dwarven armies ended it. They leveled not only elven cities but every tree in their path, and what grew back in the centuries since has been grass. The elves have not forgotten the forest. They have simply learned to live without it.

The grasslands are not a wilderness. They are a civilization that has absorbed catastrophe and continued. The peoples of Alworum Nauroytaira are known across Estaea for their hospitality, their generosity, and their mastery of horses and cattle. The animals bred here, particularly the horses, are considered among the finest in the world: the elves regard horse breeding as an art rather than a trade, managing bloodlines through families that have held the practice for a thousand years. These horses are occasionally sold to human clients, but an elf will not part with one if they believe the buyer will mistreat it.

The grasslands are also home to the Tower of Eight, the foremost center of magical study on the continent. Students from every nation are welcome; the Tower’s practitioners are renowned for their healing abilities, which are offered without charge. Admission brings obligations: apprentices are expected to remain at the Tower through their training, and those elevated to Mage status remain longer still. The Tower asks that its students set aside family and outside connections for the duration.

### A Nation Built on Loss
Alworum Nauroytaira rose from the wreckage of the Dwarven War. What had once been a great and ancient elven civilization was reduced to scattered survivors; the cities that remained were shells of what they had been, and many were not rebuilt at all. The three human tribes who arrived from across the sea found an elven nation weakened and wary. The Meresagians proved different in character from the other newcomers, and it was with them that the elves eventually forged the alliance that has shaped both peoples since. The elves of Alworum Nauroytaira would not be absorbed into human society; what they built with Meresaw was a partnership, not a merger, and both sides have been careful to remember the distinction.

### People and Culture
Elven homes in the grasslands are built from stone and, along the river districts, bamboo: practical materials for a people who rebuilt from grassland and riverbank. The elves are devoted to Hirnaloyta above all other gods, a devotion shaped by the history of what her forests meant and what their destruction cost. The art, the music, and the storytelling of the culture all return to this inheritance.

Human visitors are received with genuine warmth, though access to elven cities is not unconditional. Visitors may remain for up to a week without special permission; longer stays require approval from the relevant council. The elves are not hostile to humans, but they are deliberate about the terms of coexistence.

### Cities of the Grasslands
**Baelessaira — **The capital is a symbol of elven resilience, built and rebuilt around the single structure that survived the Dwarven War intact: the Temple of Hirnaloyta, now known as Hirnaloyta’s Promise. The original elven stonework, considered a masterpiece of the golden age of elvenkind, has been woven over the centuries into a living structure of vines and branches by generations of elven gardeners; the boundary between the ancient stone and the living growth is no longer easy to find, which is understood to be the point. Reaching the temple’s center requires navigating a many-layered labyrinth of shaped vegetation. On certain nights, bard-priests gather at bonfires in the clearings around the labyrinth to sing of the Dwarven War, the fires lit with alchemical powders that shift their color and shape to illustrate the telling. The city beyond the temple is built from stone and, along the river districts, bamboo. Population: approximately 200,000.

**Melvantharis —** One of the oldest elven cities in the world, Melvantharis sits in a mountain range at the eastern edge of the grasslands. It survived the Dwarven War only barely, and what was rebuilt is magnificent and largely empty; there are significantly more houses than families, and elves of pure cultural heritage are offered incentives to occupy abandoned homes. The ancient white marble construction is precise and cool, some of it carved directly into the mountainside and integrated with underground rivers considered sacred. The city governs itself through a body called the City Circle, which has administered Melvantharis for centuries in the absence of a surviving royal line. Its politics are hardline by elven standards: human visitors are permitted but closely supervised, and those who come to stay do so under strict conditions and with the understanding that they will not leave. Melvantharis trades in marble and wood sculptures, alchemical herbs, and, increasingly, weapons. Population: approximately 200,000.
 
## THE ECHARTEAN EMPIRE
Echartea is a large island off the southeastern coast of the mainland, the seat of an empire that extends through a chain of islands running south into tropical waters. Mainlanders who have never visited know it primarily through its ships and its merchants: the Echartesh are the dominant naval power in the known world, and their vessels are unmistakable.

Echartesh ships are enormous, colorful, and faster than anything their size has any right to be. They come in several configurations but share a distinctive aesthetic that makes them identifiable at distance; a sailor who has seen one Echartesh vessel can recognize the next without difficulty. They are capable of navigating the Elisumnaessavia River as far inland as the edge of Llewelyn Wood, which gives the Echartesh reach into the continent’s interior that no other sea power can match.

### Faith and the Sea
Enschede, the goddess of fate, is the patron deity of Echartea, and the relationship between the Echartesh and their goddess shapes the culture at every level. The sea is understood as Enschede’s most direct expression in the material world: unforgiving, indifferent, capable of damning or rewarding without explanation. A people who have built their civilization on it have absorbed those qualities into their own character.

No Echartesh vessel sails without a Herald of Enschede aboard. The Heralds are trained navigators and devout priests, responsible for reading winds and currents and ensuring the ship holds its best course. Service at sea is a prerequisite for advancement within the Temple; an Alryayi, the high priests of Enschede, will have spent years on the water before reaching that rank.

The prohibition most distinctive to Echartesh religious practice is the ban on divination. Foreknowledge of coming events is considered a direct taunt to Enschede, an assertion that a mortal can see what fate has arranged and thereby sidestep it. The Alryayi enforce this within their temples with considerable thoroughness. Visitors to Echartea who practice or seek divination are advised to keep that fact to themselves.

### People and Culture
The Echartesh are tropical in climate and appearance, dressing in bright, loosely fitted lightweight fabrics suited to the heat and humidity of their islands. Tattooing, piercing, and scarring are common forms of personal ornamentation, their patterns carrying social and religious meaning that outsiders rarely learn to read accurately.
Echartesh cities are built from stone, brick, and adobe, their architecture marked by minarets, domes, arches, and columns. Buildings are typically flat-roofed, the upper surfaces given over to gardens and outdoor living. Coastal buildings use half-round roof tiles to manage the frequent and sometimes torrential rains.

Outside their own empire, the Echartesh appear primarily as traders. They control the spice trade of the southern islands and supply luxurious fabrics, exotic dyes, and animals to the mainland markets. They are also significant participants in the slave trade, supplying considerable demand in both Bellicose and Coudassis. Echartesh merchants speak all the major mainland languages fluently; Echartean itself is rarely encountered on the continent outside of Echartesh company.
