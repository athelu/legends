# Languages of Athelu

When the three tribes of men found the shores of Estaea, they brought with them the remnants of worlds that no longer existed. Among those remnants were their words. Three peoples arrived speaking three different tongues, and none of them could understand the others.

In the ages since, two more human peoples have added their voices to the continent, and the elves who survived the Dwarven Wars brought their own tongue into the human nations that sheltered them. To speak with someone from a distant nation is to learn something difficult, and a well-traveled merchant or diplomat carries that labor as a mark of their experience.

There is no universal language in Estaea. There never was. Common ground between peoples must be built word by word.


## The Human Tongues

The six national languages of Estaea share no common ancestor. Each arrived with its people from a different place, in a different age, carrying different histories within its sounds and structures. They do not share scripts. A merchant who reads Meresagian fluently cannot puzzle out a Bellicosian contract even if she recognizes a borrowed word or two. The written forms of these languages are as foreign to one another as the nations that produced them.

Borrowed words do exist. Centuries of trade, diplomacy, and proximity leave traces. A Mersagian port city may have absorbed Coudassian terms for certain goods. A border town might use an Echartean word for a specific custom that has no equivalent at home. But these borrowings are scattered and inconsistent and they do not make languages intelligible to one another, and they are rarely written the same way twice.

### Meresagian

The language of the Kingdom of Meresaw, Meresagian is the tongue most commonly encountered by travelers moving through the heart of Estaea. The kingdom's long history of trade, its alliance with the new Elven Kingdom, and its absorption of the Odani and Urjack peoples have left the tongue thick with loanwords and to outsiders, somewhat unpredictable.

Those who learn Meresagian often find it contains words for which other languages have no equivalent, with no obvious source to trace them back to.

### Coudassian

The Coudassians landed in the west and built an empire. Their language reflects this: Coudassian is structured, formal in its written registers, and carries a long tradition of legal and bureaucratic precision. Coudassian scholars and barristers are well regarded across Estaea, and a document written in Coudassian carries an implicit weight of institutional authority, even among people who cannot read it.

The spoken language varies considerably by region, with the southern dialects carrying influences from long maritime contact with peoples beyond Estaea's coasts. The written standard, however, has remained largely stable for centuries — a point of pride among Coudassian scholars and a source of quiet frustration for everyone else.

### Bellikoz

The Bellicosians call their language *Bellikoz*, the warrior's tongue, and it earns the name. Bellicosian culture folds military rank and religious hierarchy together with civic obligation in ways most outsiders find difficult to untangle, and the vocabulary reflects this: single words carry distinctions that speakers of other languages need whole phrases to express.

The Bellicosian writing system, called Bellihagga, runs left to right and bears no resemblance to the scripts of neighboring nations. Its angular, structured characters were designed, according to Bellicosian tradition, for legibility under field conditions, made to be cut or stamped rather than carefully drawn.

The Bellicosian practice of Ja'Alkira, the singular devotion to Alkira above all other gods, has given the language a rich theological vocabulary found nowhere else in Estaea. Terms like *Y'Ashan* and *N'Ashan*, distinguishing the warrior-priests who rain fire from those who heal and restore, exist only in Bellikoz.

### Echartean

The Echartesh settled in a land shaped by deep gorges, hidden valleys, and the ley lines that converge beneath the mountains. Their language carries something of that topography — it is precise about hidden things, careful in its distinctions, and traditionally associated with scholarship and the esoteric. The priests of Enschede, the goddess of fate, are often Echartean, and the language has an unusually refined vocabulary for prophecy, probability, and the nature of knowledge itself.

Echartean names and place names tend toward sounds that feel old even when they are not. Outsiders often describe the spoken language as measured and formal, though Echartesh who know the tongue well will point out that its informalities are simply more carefully concealed than those of other nations.

### Odani

The Odani people, the Reshi and Tandu tribes, inhabit the southern coast of what is now the Meresaw Kingdom, and their language predates their absorption into that kingdom by centuries. Odani is an oral tradition first and a written one second; its literature lives primarily in sung and spoken forms, and the written script was developed relatively late, partially in response to contact with Meresagian administrative systems.

Odani encodes the speaker's relationship to the person addressed and the circumstances of the exchange directly into its grammar. Speaking it poorly marks a person as someone who does not understand the situation they are in, which among the Odani tends to matter more than simply being foreign.

### Urjack

The Urjack inhabit the Lackabier peaks and the high country of eastern Estaea. Their culture is built around endurance in isolated communities, and the language reflects this: Urjack has an unusually large vocabulary for weather and terrain, with fine distinctions between types of snowfall or gradations of cold that speakers of other languages typically render with a single word.

Urjack is rarely written outside of the peaks. Literacy is primarily the domain of clan record-keepers, and an Urjack trader in a lowland city will typically have learned the local tongue. Urjack is rarely spoken outside of the peaks.


### Hadriaeth

The language of the Haiden elves is called Hadriaeth, a word that, in its own tongue, simply means *language*. The elves once assumed their tongue was the tongue, the one against which others were measured. The Dwarven Wars and the centuries that followed have worn that assumption down, but something of it lingers.

Hadriaeth is an ancient tongue. Its most distinctive sound to outside ears is a lateral fricative — the *ll*, like the Welsh *llan* — that runs through elven speech and marks it as recognizable before a word is fully spoken. Stress falls on the penultimate syllable, giving the language a characteristic lilt that human learners often struggle to sustain.

The writing system is called Saitana, meaning *The Gods Return* in Hadriaeth, and runs from right to left, then top to bottom, in a flowing cursive script with no resemblance to any human writing system. A scholar who has spent years on Meresagian and Coudassian picks up a Hadriaeth text and will find nothing familiar.

Haiden elves raised within human nations typically speak the language of that nation as their primary tongue. For many, Hadriaeth is a heritage language, present in the household, in ritual, in the names of things, but not the tongue of daily life. How much survives depends on the family.

The new Elven Kingdom, rebuilt east of the Lackabier peak, has worked to restore Hadriaeth as a living language for its people. Within its borders it is the language of governance and education; Meresagian serves for trade and diplomacy, but a visitor who arrives speaking only Meresagian will find themselves at a disadvantage in the interior.


### Dwarven

Dwarven is a dead language. The dwarves who spoke it are gone from the world. What remains of the tongue is carved inscriptions on ancient stonework and the academic texts that scholars used to piece its grammar back together from those inscriptions.

To learn Dwarven is to learn the language of a civilization that destroyed itself and nearly took the elves with it. Scholars who specialize in it are a small and slightly peculiar community, drawn to a tongue that opens onto a world no one alive has seen.

The practical value of Dwarven is narrow: it is useful for reading what the dwarves left behind, and for little else. For the historians and archaeologists who need it, that is reason enough.

Universities in larger cities include it in their curricula. The language is not secret or prohibited, it simply requires finding someone capable of teaching it, and such people are not common.
